About the Story

When Nereus, a high-ranking soldier of the small Lestros empire, ventures into the forest to hunt for his starving city, he finds himself captured by the heir to the Norlan throne—the formidable Prince Cassianus. But Cassianus does not simply take him prisoner. He takes everything.

In a world where a single kiss from a foreigner brands you a traitor, Nereus finds himself caught between loyalty, identity, and a curse he never asked for. The Curse of Norlan is a tale of honor, survival, and the strange ties that bind enemies together.

Chapter 1

Warning: This chapter contains self-harm.

Nereus stood as quietly as possible, hiding behind a tree, knowing the Norlans before him were too many to defeat. Nereus remembered his father’s words, “Child, always remember this: if you ever kiss a foreigner, willingly or unwillingly, you will be marked as a traitor by the Lestrosan community and will be given the death penalty. A kiss from a foreigner means that you can never return to Lestros. It means that even I can’t love you anymore.”

Nereus was no ordinary warrior; he was a high-ranking soldier in the small Lestros empire. He was a handsome man. He had a slightly dark complexion, light blue eyes, and black hair. An old scar ran from his right eye to his upper lip.

But the company in front of him was no circus; it consisted of ten heavily armed soldiers, among them, a bronze princely chariot being pulled by four majestic white stallions. On the chariot stood the heir to the Norlan throne, the one and only, Prince Visuvian Cassianus Norlan.

The rumours of this man lurking in these forests had terrified the Lestrosans so much that no one dared to go out to hunt, driving the city toward famine. The Lestrosan King sent his best soldier, Nereus, to bring food. So he ventured into the forest to find food.

Cassianus was adjusting his gold armour. When one of his soldiers asked him, “Why are we here?”

“Soldier, the war god has said the name Nereus. My sister needs him, or we all die,” replied Cassianus. Cassianus was the most muscular and tallest person Nereus had ever seen, in contrast to Nereus’s feminine body. Nereus absolutely hated his physical features, but what he lacks in size, he makes up for with speed and agility.

Nereus was camouflaged, but fate betrayed him: a sudden cramp seized his left thigh. Out of all times, his body decided to betray him now. Nereus felt excruciating pain, yet he remained surreptitious; the pain became unbearable, so Nereus moved his leg just a bit. No normal eyesight would have spotted the movement, but Cassianus’s sharp eyesight did, and he threw one of his spears at the tree. The spear pierced the tree’s trunk and barely grazed Nereus’s head; it was just a show of strength on Cassianus’s part.

Cassianus commanded eight of his men to attack and capture the Lestrosan. Eight soldiers raced forward, forming a defensive wall using their shields. One of the soldiers shot an arrow at Nereus. Nereus effortlessly deflected it with his sword. Then all the soldiers drew their weapons and lunged at Nereus.

The first soldier to attack Nereus had a giant sword; his sword was bigger than Nereus’s. Nereus feigned to the soldier’s left, to which the soldier committed, allowing Nereus to finish him off. Nereus blocked a stab from another soldier. The soldier in front of Nereus carried a long trident. He was the only soldier who kept up with Nereus’s strikes and feints.

While Nereus was busy fighting the soldier, yet another soldier lunged at Nereus, but Nereus was still calm and composed, saving energy for the fight against Cassianus. Nereus kicked the soldier’s breastplate, causing the soldier to stumble. Nereus did not get time to finish him, as two other soldiers tried to slash at him. Nereus blocked their attack and then slashed his sword at the two other soldiers with such immense speed that both soldiers were caught off guard, and they dropped dead.

The soldier, who had stumbled, got up quickly and stabbed Nereus’s flank using his dagger, right where the armour left space for movement. His abdomen filled with pain, which only caused Nereus to get angrier. With one swift swing, the soldier was dead, and then another soldier faced the same fate as his dead comrades. One by one, the rest of the eight soldiers perished.

Nereus removed his helmet, smirking at Cassianus, as if killing eight Norlan soldiers had been nothing more than a warm-up.

The two other soldiers felt rage at seeing Nereus’s smug face and charged at him, but were stopped in their tracks by Cassianus, who used his spear to block their way. Cassianus threw back his head and laughed, ”Well done, Lestrosan, you have managed to pique my interest.”

Nereus, frowning, put on his helmet and got ready for battle. The pain in his abdomen had dulled, but his abdomen screamed with every movement, reminding him of his mistake.

Cassianus noticed Nereus’s unease and offered, “You are injured. After I capture you, the Lestrosans will claim that I could only defeat you because you were injured, while in reality, you were just weak. Come to my palace and heal. Then fight me.”

Nereus scoffed, “First vanquish me, then talk.” Nereus knew that he had a better chance of defeating Cassianus here than on his doorstep.

“As you wish,” Cassianus said in mock flattery. Cassianus picked up a blood-red spear. Then he advanced.

Nereus lifted his sword and charged. He swung the sword at Cassianus’s neck. Cassianus blocked the attack with his spear; the force of the impact sent both fighters a step back. This time, Cassianus charged, and Nereus was surprised by Cassianus’s speed. Cassianus kept attacking with increasing speed. Nereus was forced to play defence as Cassianus was not even within his range. Nereus then noticed an opening and slashed at Cassianus’s neck. His sword missed the gap and struck Cassianus’s helmet, and sparks flew. Cassianus smacked Nereus right on his wound using the shaft of his spear.

Nereus’s abdominal pain exploded, and his breath grew heavier. He switched his sword to his left hand and pulled out a magnificent sword from his second scabbard. He sneered, “Remember this blade?”

Cassianus’s stance changed; his voice grew angrier, “My uncle’s sword. You dare wield a Norlan sword against me.”

Nereus icily said, “I don’t draw this blade for pests.” Nereus got up and blitzed towards Cassianus.

Nereus attacked with immense speed, and Cassianus was forced into defence. This was it, thought Nereus; all he had to do was kill Cassianus before he adapted to Nereus’s dual-wielding. Cassianus was lagging, and then Nereus saw an opening. He jumped into the air, both swords above him and then brought them down on Cassianus’s head, but his wound slowed him down.

Cassianus blocked both swords with the shaft of his spear and then kicked Nereus right in the wound. “You are slow,” sneered Cassianus.

Cassianus then did not allow Nereus a breather, and while Nereus struggled in pain, Cassianus swung his spear like a bat and struck Nereus’s head. Then Cassianus placed his spear’s cold tip on Nereus’s neck, and then he reached for Bellatoris Maximi. He inspected the blade and said, “I will be taking this if you don’t mind.”

Nereus did not reply. The sword did not matter anymore.

Cassianus then called to his men, “Throw this Lestrosan into the cage,” he commanded. The soldiers grabbed and hoisted Nereus onto their shoulders. They brought him to the chariot and put him in the cage.

Cassianus looked at Nereus and smirked. While still looking at Nereus, he ordered one of his soldiers, “Kiss him!”

“But I am not–” protested the soldier.

“Do it,” thundered Cassianus, making the soldier whimper in fear.

“On the lips?”

“Yes!”

The man quickly bent down. As he crept closer, Nereus skipped a beat; he had not expected the Norlan Prince to know Lestrosan customs. Nereus felt like pleading, begging for mercy. He wanted to cry till Cassianus felt pity, but he was no peasant. So, Nereus held his head high, not giving Cassianus the satisfaction of watching him grovel and plead.

The man finally kissed Nereus. It was quick and cold and meant nothing romantically, but Nereus’s fate was sealed. Nereus’s eyes became watery, and tears fell from his eyes. It felt like dying; no, it was worse: his father’s voice in his head, “…even I can’t love you anymore.”

Cassianus lifted Nereus’s head using the butt-cap of his spear. He took off his helmet, revealing a perfect, unscarred face. Blonde hair framed his head. He looked at Nereus with his green eyes. His voice broke, “I am so sorry.”

Cassianus rubbed his eyes. Cassianus put on his helmet, mounted the chariot, and urged it forward. “Let’s go home,” he said to his horses.

Nereus could not understand why the man who captured him was crying. Nereus was crying. He composed himself and noticed that one of the metal bars of the cage had snapped, revealing a jagged and sharpened edge. Nereus got an idea, after all, death is better than being a captive. Nereus aligned his body with the pole and closed his eyes. Nereus then drove the jagged metal into his chest. As he became unconscious, a smile of freedom and relief appeared on his face.

But ironically, Nereus had missed all of his vital organs. As a result, while Nereus believed he had been granted freedom by death, he was unaware that he remained captive to this weird prince.

Chapter 2

Nereus’s head hurt as his eyes opened. Nereus felt confused. Why was he in the middle of nowhere, wearing a purple robe that was too big for him? Nereus shook his head and then saw Cassianus sitting right there beside him. He suddenly remembered his capture.

Cassianus said, “Rest, your chest is not in good shape, you are lucky that you —”

Nereus grabbed Cassianus’s royal robe’s collar and shouted at him, “What’s your problem? Once you are prideful, then you are sorry, and now you're being kind to me. I am so unlucky that I even failed to die.”

Nereus and Cassianus were face-to-face; Cassianus’s warm breath hit Nereus’s face. Cassianus slapped away Nereus’s hand. “Let go of my gold robe. You should have been careful about where you stabbed yourself.”

Nereus shouted, “What, careful? I was trying to die, dummy.”

Cassianus tutted, “You do not know what I am talking about, do you?” Then he came closer to Nereus’s shoulder, his nose scraped Nereus’s ear, and he whispered in a voice that gave Nereus goosebumps, “I know about your little secret.”

Nereus’s eyes widened. His dignity was already low enough, but if his secret became public, people would look at him with more disgust than when they look at a thief. No, it can’t be; Nereus did not believe it; he was now physically and mentally being controlled by Cassianus. Nereus whispered back, “And, are you going to tell somebody?”

“Not if you co-operate, slave.”

Nereus was stunted. How could his captor change his attitude so fast? But Nereus was not one to change personality; he grumbled, “Slave, I am no slave.”

Cassianus slapped Nereus; Nereus’s cheek exploded with pain. Cassianus stood beside him, looking at the fallen Nereus, “That attitude of yours has to change, slave.”

Nereus stayed on the ground and looked at the sand.

Cassianus then softened; his attitude changed like the tides. He warmly said in a friendly manner, “That is more like it. Rest; once you heal, we will set course to Eryndor, the capital of Norlan.”

After Nereus healed, they again started their journey towards Eryndor. Nereus had received better arrangements this time, with his hands and legs tied to a really comfy chair. Cassianus looked at Nereus and said, “It is time you know your fate.”
